# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Construction business owners and project managers, often the same person in a small-to-medium company. They're running the whole operation simultaneously: quoting incoming leads, coordinating tradies across active builds, tracking milestone progress, reviewing financials, and handling government compliance. The job context is high-stakes and time-pressured. They're glancing at a dashboard between site visits, not sitting at a desk for 8 hours.

## Product Purpose

End-to-end construction management for the people who build things. Covers the full operational arc: lead capture, quoting, project execution, tradie scheduling, milestone tracking, financials, and government compliance. The chatbot layer adds AI-assisted lookups and workflow shortcuts. Success looks like a business owner who closes the day knowing exactly where every job stands without chasing down information.

## Brand Personality

Sharp, modern, confident. Ambitious without being flashy. A tier above what the construction industry expects from its software, but never precious about it. The tool is there to serve the work, not to perform.

## References

Linear: precise, fast, opinionated. Strong visual choices that never feel decorative. Loom / Superhuman: consumer-grade polish applied to a productivity tool. High craft, high speed, treats the user as someone with taste.

## Anti-references

Generic SaaS (Asana, Monday.com): pastel cards, bouncy animations, startup-template feel. Construction businesses aren't project management startups.

Old-school construction software (Procore, BuilderTrend): dense, grey, utilitarian, enterprise-circa-2015. This should look like it was built this decade.

Finance and banking dark mode: terminal-native, crypto-adjacent, neon accents on black. Precision is correct; the aesthetic register is wrong. BuildPro operates in daylight, not a trading floor at midnight.

## Design Language

Operations-centre aesthetic. Lean, fast, and data-first. The interface has the quiet confidence of a tool that has been through a hundred difficult jobs and knows what it's doing. Every screen prioritises information over decoration. Visual weight goes to the data, not the chrome.

Think Linear or Stripe Dashboard applied to construction: high information density, restrained palette, purposeful motion, no decorative flourish. The UI should feel designed by engineers who build things, not by designers who make things look nice.

Not the dark-and-moody finance aesthetic. Not the airy minimalism of a consumer productivity tool. Not the heavy enterprise chrome of legacy construction software. The gap between those three failures is where BuildPro lives.

## Design Principles

**Operator-grade density.** Every element on screen earns its place by carrying operational meaning. Information is primary. Decoration does not exist unless it serves the data.

**Confident, not safe.** Strong visual choices over restrained defaults. The interface looks intentional, not like a template dropped into an industry vertical. Sharp without being aggressive.

**Speed as craft.** Interactions feel immediate. Latency is a design problem. Controls respond faster than the user expects. Loading states are honest; they never spin longer than necessary.

**One tool, many hats.** A business owner and a project manager might be the same person switching context mid-session. Workflows don't force a single role lens; they surface what's needed for the current task without requiring mode-switching.

**Industry-intelligent.** The UI understands construction context. Terminology, workflows, and data shapes reflect how the industry actually operates. This is not a generic SaaS with a construction skin applied.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA. Icon-only controls need `aria-label`. Status is never conveyed by color alone when a label can be explicit. Focus rings remain visible. Dialogs and drawers preserve keyboard navigation and escape handling.
